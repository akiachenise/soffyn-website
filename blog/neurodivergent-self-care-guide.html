Real Self-Care for Neurodivergent Women: What Actually Helps When You're Burned Out — And What My Therapist Told Me

April 2026 7 min read By Akia

My therapist said something to me a few years ago that I keep coming back to. I had just finished explaining my self-care routine — the one I'd been trying to maintain, the one I felt guilty about not doing consistently. She listened to all of it and then asked: "Who told you rest was something you had to earn?"

I didn't have an answer. I just sat there. Because I genuinely couldn't trace it back to a single source — it felt like it had come from everywhere, from school and work and family and every wellness article I'd ever read. The message that your worth is tied to your output. That rest is a reward for productivity, not a basic human requirement.

That conversation changed a lot for me. Not immediately, and not easily. But it was the first time someone in a professional setting gave me permission to question a framework I'd built my entire approach to "taking care of myself" around. If you've never had that conversation — this is me having it with you.

Why the Standard Playbook Doesn't Stick

I have a complicated relationship with self-care advice. Not because the concept is wrong, but because most of the advice assumes a brain I don't have. Light a candle. Take a bath. Set aside thirty minutes for yourself every day. Do the routine until it becomes automatic.

These things are offered like they're obvious, like the only obstacle between you and self-care is not having tried hard enough. But for a brain with ADHD, every step in that chain is its own obstacle. Planning the thirty minutes requires executive function. Remembering it requires working memory. Starting it requires overcoming initiation resistance. By the time you've mentally geared up for self-care, you've already burned through the resources you were trying to restore.

And then when you miss a day, the guilt hits. Because the implicit message in "do this every day" is that missing means failing. For Black neurodivergent women who are already navigating constant pressure to be disciplined, capable, and never visibly struggling — one more space in your life where you're falling short is not rest. It's just a different kind of depletion.

What My Therapist Actually Taught Me About Rest

Over time, my therapist helped me understand something that sounds simple but took me years to really land: rest is not the absence of doing. It's the presence of restoration. And what restores one nervous system is not what restores another.

For her other clients, rest might mean stillness and silence. For my brain — and maybe yours — stillness isn't rest. It's agitation in a quiet container. My brain keeps moving. It keeps generating. It doesn't stop because I told it to or because I positioned myself on a couch with a candle lit.

She also said something about shame and self-care that I've repeated probably a hundred times since: "You can't relax your way into self-care while you're berating yourself for not doing it right." The guilt around missing the routine was costing more energy than the routine was ever giving back. The self-criticism was the problem, not the inconsistency.

"You can't relax your way into self-care while you're berating yourself for not doing it right."

What Rest Actually Looks Like for My Brain

Rest for me looks like movement sometimes. A walk with music I love. Dancing around my kitchen at 10pm because something needs to move through me. Something physical that processes whatever has built up from the day. It looks like hyperfocusing on something I actually enjoy — not productive, not goal-oriented, just following a thread of genuine interest somewhere without watching the clock. It looks like stimming, which I didn't have a name for until adulthood but which is just my nervous system doing what it does to regulate. The repetitive behaviors I was taught to suppress turned out to be exactly what my brain uses to settle.

The permission I had to give myself, and that I want to offer you: if it settles your system, it counts. You don't need it to look like what gets photographed in wellness content. You need it to actually work for you.

Journaling Without Making It a Whole Thing

I have bought many beautiful notebooks. For years I would write in them three or four times and then they'd go on a shelf as a monument to good intentions. The notebooks weren't the problem. The format was.

What I actually needed was permission to journal in a way that matched how my brain works — scattered, nonlinear, often not wanting to write complete sentences. A voice note while I'm driving counts. A photo of a color that captures how I'm feeling counts. Three words typed into my phone because something needs to get out of my head before I can sleep — that counts too. The point is expression, not performance. The point is getting something out of you so you're not carrying it everywhere.

When I stopped making journaling mean a notebook and a half hour blocked off, I actually started doing it. Messy, inconsistent, formless — and genuinely useful.

Affirmations That Don't Feel Like Lying

My therapist introduced me to something she called "bridging statements" — the idea that if a positive affirmation is too far from where you actually are, your brain rejects it. "I am enough" makes some people cringe not because they're negative but because their brain catches the gap between the statement and their current felt reality, and it files the whole practice under dishonesty.

What works instead is smaller and truer. "I'm still here." "I did hard things today." "I'm allowed to rest." Not aspirational — just accurate, or permission-giving. Telling myself I'm allowed to stop is more useful to me than telling myself I'm thriving, because the thing blocking me most of the time isn't a lack of confidence. It's a lack of permission to be a regular, inconsistent, imperfect human being. The affirmation I actually need is the one that addresses what's really in the way.

Imperfect Self-Care Is Still Self-Care

Here's another thing my therapist said that I didn't believe at first: a five-minute walk is not a lesser version of a thirty-minute one. It's just a five-minute walk. It still counts. It still matters to your nervous system. The idea that effort only counts if it's sustained and consistent is a productivity myth that has no business living inside your self-care practice.

One journal entry, one walk, one time you said no to something that was going to cost you energy you didn't have — these are real. Calling them insufficient is just one more way we refuse to give ourselves credit for what we're doing to survive. And most of us are doing a lot more than we give ourselves credit for.

Building a Toolkit, Not a Routine

What I have now, instead of a routine, is a loose collection of things I know work for me — not every day, not in any particular order, not with any expectation that I use all of them or any of them consistently. Sensory things that settle me. Ways to move that feel good. Music. Specific content that helps me decompress without adding noise. Ways of being with people and ways of being alone. Grounding practices I can do in two minutes when I need to come back to my body.

A toolkit is different from a routine because it carries no expectations. It doesn't care what order you use it in or whether you use it every day. It's just available when you need it. And that low-barrier, no-judgment availability is exactly what works for executive function struggles — the thing that requires the least activation energy is the thing you'll actually reach for when you're depleted.

You don't need a better routine. You need a framework built for how your brain actually works. And permission — real permission, from someone who means it — to take care of yourself the way you actually can.
